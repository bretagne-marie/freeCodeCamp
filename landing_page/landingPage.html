Joséphine Baker

The coveted black dancer, actress, singer, civil rights activist, and bisexual icon who helped win the French Resistance

A Brief Timeline of Joséphine Baker's Life

  • 1906 - Born on 3 June in St. Louis, MO as Freda J. Mcdonald
  • 1914 - At only 8-years-old, began working as a live-in domestic worker for white families, some of which were physically abusive towards her
  • 1918 - Dropped out of school at 12
  • 1919 - Began working as a waitress at the Old Chauffeur's Club, while also living in cardboard shelters and scavenging for food in garbage cans in the slums of St. Louis. Also made money doing street-corner dancing. Was also briefly married to her first husband
  • 1921 - Married her second husband at 15, Willie Baker, whose surname she continued to use professionally for the rest of her life
  • 1921 cont. - Left Willie when her vaudeville troupe, the St. Louis Chorus, was booked to perform in New York City during the Harlem Renaissance. Performed as the last dancer on the end of the chorus line - which not only requires good rhythym and vocals, but comedic chops too - for groundbreaking and hugely successful Broadway revues like Shuffle Along (1921) and The Chocolate Dandies (1924). She was considered the "highest paid chrous girl in vaudeville" during this time
  • 1925 - Sailed to Paris at age 19, where she would live for the rest of her life. Opened in Le Revue Négre at the Théâtre des Champs-Élysées
  • 1926 - Became an instant success for her erotic dancing, and for appearing nude onstage. Starred at the Folies Bergère and also performed her notorious "Danse Sauvage" or banana dance at the Exposition internationale des Arts décoratifs
  • 1931 - Scored her most successful song, "J'ai deux amours"
  • 1934 - Starred in the French film Zouzou, making her the first black woman to headline a major motion picture
  • 1937 - married French industrialist Jean Lion and became a French citizen
  • 1939 - recruited by the French military intelligence agency to collect information about German troop locations from officials she met at parties. She specialized in gatherings at embassies and ministries. Her café-society fame enabled her to charm people like high-ranking Japanese officials or Italian bureaucrats and she reported back what she heard without raising suspicion. She carried information for transmission to England about airfields, harbors, and German troop concentrations in the West of France. She wrote her notes in invisible ink on her sheet music
  • 1939 cont. - Met then had a brief relationship with artist Frida Kahlo
  • 1945 - Received the Croix de guerre and the Rosette de la Résistance. Was also made a Chevalier of the Légion d'honneur by General Charles de Gaulle
  • 1951 - Toured the US and helped desegregrate nightclub audiences in Miami and Las Vegas. Began her work with the NAACP and was also named their "Woman of the Year." They also declared 20 May as "Josephine Baker Day"
  • 1963 - Was the only headlining female speaker at the March on Washington. Gave a speech at the side of Dr. Martin Luther King Jr.
  • 1975 - starred in a retrospective revue at the Bobino in Paris, Joséphine à Bobino 1975, that celebrated her 50 years in show business. The revue was notably financed by Prince Rainier of Monaco and his wife Princess Grace Kelly, as well as Jacqueline Kennedy Onassis. The opening night audience included stars like Sophia Loren, Mick Jagger, Shirley Bassey, Diana Ross, and Liza Minelli
  • 1975 cont. - Four days later, Baker was found lying peacefully in her bed surrounded by newspapers with glowing reviews of her performance. She was in a coma after suffering a cerebral hemorrhage. She eventually passed away, aged 68, on 12 April. She is the only American woman to receive full French military honors at her funeral

"I have walked into the palaces of kings and queens and into the houses of presidents. And much more. But I could not walk into a hotel in America and get a cup of coffee, and that made me mad. And when I get mad, you know that I open my big mouth. And then look out, ’cause when Josephine opens her mouth, they hear it all over the world...."

-- Joséphine Baker at the March on Washington
